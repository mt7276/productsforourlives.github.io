How to choose a radar gun

When buying a baseball radar gun or speed sensor, you should always buy one to fit your needs. A parent or little league coach is probably not going to need the same advanced version of a radar gun as someone coaching higher levels or doing scouting. A baseball radar gun is also not just for baseball. They can be used for softball, tennis lacrosse, hockey or any sport where you need to track speed. Below are some things to look for when selecting a radar gun.

  • Price - Stay within you budget. Radar guns can range from around $100 up into the thousands.
  • Size - Do you want something larger that may be a little more difficult to transport or one that can fit in your pocket? This is more of a personal preference, because size doesn't make too much of a difference in accuracy or quality.
  • Accurcacy - Most radar guns will track speeds at +/- 1 MPH(+/- 2 km/h). Some are just more consistent in doing so. Read product reviews and compare to other models to find what you need.
  • Range - See how far the radar gun can get speeds from. If you know how far away you will be when tracking speeds, look for a radar gun that will gather readings at that distance.
  • Speed Tracking - Radar guns all have minimum and maximium speeds they can track. Be sure to choose one that is right for your needs. Make sure the radar gun has the speed you plan on reading within it's range.
  • Operation - Do you want a radar gun or speed sensor that can be used by one person or will you be using it to measure other peoples speeds?
  • Sport - Most radar guns can be used for baseball, softball, tennis or any sport. But some are sport specific. Get a radar gun that will do what you need it to do.
  • Battery - Check the battery life of the radar gun and the type of battery used.

More details of the radar guns I have experience using

Bushnell Velocity Speed Gun

The Bushnell Velocity Speed Gun is the one that I personally own and have used numerous times in the yard and at the park to track baseball and softball speeds. It is very simple to use, just aim and pull and hold the trigger. Once the trigger is released, it will display the fastest speed on the screen. When getting a thrown ball speed, I hold the gun and have the kids throw to me or I stand behind the person catching. To get exit velocity off the bat, I stand behind a net and have the batter hit towards me. I capture speeds this way because I seem to get better readings when I am in line with the path of the ball and not off to the side. Occasionally I will get a reading that seems off, but for the most part it reads pretty consistently. The gun can track ball speeds from 10 - 110 MPH at ranges up to 90 feet as well as getting running speeds of players. Mine has been known to get a cars speed on occasion when one is driving too fast down the street. It displays the speed on an LCD screen and will show either MPH or KM/H. Click Bushnell Velocity Speed Gun to see pricing and see all it has to offer.

Pocket Radar Ball Coach

Ball Coach is a great radar gun option offered by Pocket Radar. It will measure speeds of 25 - 130 MPH at up to 120 feet away. The velocity is displayed on the LCD screen either in MPH or KM/H. This radar is not shaped like a gun, is very small in size and can fit in the palm of your hand. The small design makes it easy to be discreet when you want to get speed readings. I have used the Ball Coach at the indoor facility my kids train at quite a few times and have zero complaints. It gives really accurate readings and I was able to get baseball exit velocities by standing behind the batter and taking readings as the ball was hit away from me. Unlike my radar gun, the pocket radar also seems to be more accurate when you are not lined up directly with the path of the baseball or softball. From what I can tell, bad readings are few and far between. Although I have not used the feature, it can store up to 25 speeds internally, which means you don't have to record the velocity as often. What you see in the image is the ball coach bundle which comes with a tripod, battery charging pack and a cleaning cloth. If you want the device only, you can go to it by clicking Ball Coach, or select the check price button to see the bundle.

Stalker Sport 2 - Scout

Stalker Sport offers top of the line radar guns, but they come at a price. I used the Stalker Sport 2 a couple times at my sons indoor baseball practice last year just for capturing pitch speed, but didn't get into many more of the features. I was clocking an ex college pitcher while he was pitching to our 8U baseball team and I will say that this radar gun never gave a reading that seemed off or inaccurate. The Sport has a 300 foot range and can capture speeds from 5 - 150 MPH. It has an LCD readout which shows the speeds in either MPH or KM/H. It will show not only the peak velocity of a throw or pitch, but also the across the plate speed.

Sports Sensors Glove Radar

The Sports Sensors Glove Radar is another option that I have been able to use first hand. My buddy Will has one and it is a pretty nice product. The device attaches to the back of the glove, sends doppler radar through the glove and gets the ball speed right before it is caught. We have compared readings from his Glove Radar and my Bushnell Radar Gun and the results are fairly similar. It is kind of hard to do a one for one comparison though, since my Bushnell records the baseballs top speed and his radar gets the speed right before it is caught. Since a baseball usually slows down about 1 MPH every 7 feet it travels, we just do the math using the thrown distance and get the top speed to compare. Even though some math is needed to get the highest speed of a baseball, this is a great device for tracking progress. If you are always at the same pitching or throwing distance when measuring, you can just compare the speed on the LCD against other throws. Using this radar seems a little safer when you do not have a third person to hold the gun because it leaves the hand not inside the glove to help catch the ball, instead of holding a radar gun. A draw back to the Glove Radar is that with younger kids, the balls are not always well thrown. If the baseball or softball isn't caught, a reading can't be taken. We can agree that this is the way it should be, but with young kids, seeing a high number on a bad throw could help them not discouraged about where the ball went. A postive for the Glove Radar would be that kids would try thier best to hit the glove so they can get their speed.

9" Speed Sensor Baseball - Markwort

The last speed radar option from above that I have used personally, is the Markwort Speed Sensor Baseball. Although I haven't used one since little league, I still remember how much fun we had using it to test our throwing and pitching speeds. The baseball itself has 8 predetermined distances it is designed to be thrown from(20',30' 40', 46', 50' 60.5', 70' and 80') and can measure speeds up to 120 MPH. On release, the speed sensor baseball begins to record the speed automatically. It is a great way to know what speed you are throwing when playing catch or pitching. The baseball is not designed to be hit, so it is unable to get exit velocity of a ball off the bat.
