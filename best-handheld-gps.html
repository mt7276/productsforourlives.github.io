üî¨ How GPS Works: The Science Behind Satellite Navigation

Global Positioning System (GPS) relies on a network of 24+ satellites orbiting Earth. Receivers calculate precise locations by measuring the time delay of signals exchanged with multiple satellites.

GPS receivers measure the distance to at least three satellites, using the known satellite positions and the speed of light to compute the user‚Äôs location via trilateration. Additional satellites improve accuracy and reliability.

Each GPS satellite carries an atomic clock synchronized to Coordinated Universal Time (UTC). The receiver compares signal timestamps to determine distances with nanosecond precision, translating to meter‚Äëlevel accuracy.

The GPS constellation‚Äôs geometry‚Äîquantified by Geometric Dilution of Precision (GDOP)‚Äîaffects accuracy. A wider spread of satellites in the sky lowers GDOP, yielding more precise position fixes.

For sub‚Äëmeter accuracy, Differential GPS (DGPS) uses ground reference stations to broadcast real‚Äëtime corrections. Augmentation systems like WAAS and EGNOS further enhance positioning for aviation and surveying.
