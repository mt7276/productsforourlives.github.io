Early Days: Baseball Without Speed Tracking

Before radar guns entered the scene, baseball pitchers were judged primarily by the **eye test** and performance statistics. Scouts and coaches could estimate a pitcher's velocity based on experience, but there was no precise way to quantify speed.

In the early 20th century, some rudimentary attempts were made to measure pitch speed using mechanical devices. One such method, used in 1912, involved **timing a baseball against a motorcycle moving at a known speed**. Another experiment in the 1930s by Dr. Harold Edgerton (a pioneer in strobe photography) attempted to measure ball speed using high-speed cameras. However, these methods were inconsistent and impractical for everyday use.

The Birth of the Radar Gun in Baseball

Radar technology was initially developed for military applications during World War II, primarily to detect aircraft and moving objects. The concept of **Doppler radar**, which measures the speed of objects based on frequency shifts, became the foundation for speed detection in law enforcement.

In the **1950s and 1960s**, police forces started using radar guns to measure vehicle speeds. Engineers soon realized that the same technology could be applied to sports, leading to the first baseball radar guns.

The **JUGS Gun**, introduced in **1975**, was the first radar gun designed specifically for baseball. Created by JUGS Sports, this handheld device used Doppler radar to measure the velocity of a baseball in real-time. It quickly became a game-changer for scouts and coaches.

The Rise of Radar Guns in Scouting and Player Development (1980s-1990s)

During the 1980s and 1990s, radar guns became a **staple in Major League Baseball (MLB) scouting**. Organizations invested heavily in the technology to analyze pitchers at all levels, from high school prospects to big-league aces.

The Technological Boom (2000s-Present)

With the explosion of analytics in baseball, radar guns have evolved from simple speed-measuring devices to advanced data-tracking tools.

By the early 2000s, **Stalker Radar Guns** emerged as the industry standard for professional baseball. These devices could measure pitch speed **from the pitcher's release point to home plate**, offering more detailed insights into velocity loss and ball movement.
