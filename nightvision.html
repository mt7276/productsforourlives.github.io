Night and Thermal Vision Devices

Night vision technology has been in existence for nearly 100 years. First introduced by the German company AEG in the 1930's, and used on German tanks in WWII. At this same time the United States was developing their own night vision which used infrared light to help the military's snipers. The early night vision devices are considered Generation 0. In the 1960's, the US Army came up with and patented night vision device that would amplify light around 1000 times. These bulky devices developed are considered Generation 1 night vision and were used in the Vietnam War. By the 1970's, Second Generation night vision was being developed. This newer generation of night vision had much better light amplification, closer to 20,000 times. It was also able to work better without moonlight, plus the image resolution was greater. The Second Generation of night vision lasted for a decade or so, but then in the late 1980's, Third Generation night vision was created. The Third Generation saw even greater image resolution, as well as better light amplification which increased to between 30,000 and 50,000 times.

Much like night vision, thermal image technology has been around for alomost 100 years. Thermal image devices use infrared radiation to generate the images that you see in the lens. The first motion camera using thermal imaging, was built by Kalman Tihanyl, a Hungarian Physicist. The camera was initially used for anti-aircraft efforts by the British army. Quickly seeing the value in thermal imaging devices, military all over began utilizing the technology. As the cameras improved, it didnt't take long for them to be used by first responders. Firefighters would use thermal imaging cameras to find people stuck in a burning bulding full of smoke. Law enforcement would use thermal technology to help find suspects and fugitives trying to evade capture.

As technology and production improved of both night vision and thermal imaging technologies, the devices became better and prices began to drop. Though still used by the military, law enforcement and firefighters, these devices began to become commercially available and bought by the public. Now used for night time adventures, hunting, surveillance and more, night vision and thermal imaging technology is being used by people all over the world.
